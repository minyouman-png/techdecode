Free AI for 52 Million People — Inside Korea's 'AI for Everyone' Project

By menew · July 16, 2026

Using ChatGPT properly costs $20 a month. The Korean government has run a different calculation. On July 13, 2026, the Ministry of Science and ICT opened the business tender for the ‘AI for Everyone’ (모두의 AI) project at the National Fiscal Strategy Meeting. The goal is simple and audacious: give all 52 million residents of South Korea a free AI chatbot with no cost and no usage caps. If it launches on schedule by year-end, Korea becomes the first G20 nation to provide universal free AI access as a national service.

No government has ever tried to roll out AI to its entire population as basic infrastructure. That makes this project a fascinating experiment — and one that carries several hard questions. Here’s what was announced, and what actually matters.

What exactly is ‘AI for Everyone’?

The service comes in two parts.

What it isWhen
General-purpose AI chatbotA conversational AI anyone can use, free and unlimited — pitched as a ChatGPT-class serviceBeta in late September → full launch by year-end
Public-service AI agentAn administrative assistant that proactively finds government benefits you qualify for, notifies you, and files the applications on your behalfLate 2026 → expanded through 2027

The timeline is tight. The tender runs for about a month, from July 13 to August 11. In August the government will select two to three private companies with the reach to serve the whole population. Beta service is slated for late September, full launch by year-end. From 2027, the plan is to evolve this into personalized agents — the “one AI agent per citizen” era.

Deputy Prime Minister and ICT Minister Bae Kyung-hoon asked citizens to “actively use AI for Everyone and help develop it together.” According to press reports, Kakao and LG Uplus have committed to bidding, while Naver, SK Telecom, and KT are considering it.

The conditions: this is welfare — and undisguised industrial policy

Read the tender requirements and the project’s real nature becomes clear. Participating companies must:

  • build the service on their own domestic AI models (meeting the sovereign foundation-model criteria) for at least 50% of it,
  • layer in at least 30% more from other Korean companies’ models,
  • and may use foreign models only for minimal functions — with foreign models banned entirely from any government-funded portion.

In other words, ‘AI for Everyone’ is a citizen welfare service and, at the same time, an industrial policy that manufactures 52-million-person demand for domestic AI models. With foreign frontier models (GPT, Claude, Gemini) effectively dominating the Korean market, the calculation is to funnel real-world traffic and learning opportunities to homegrown models.

The infrastructure support is concrete too. This year the government will allocate the 512 Nvidia B200 GPUs it has secured to the selected operators for development and operations; from next year, the operating costs of the nationwide free service will be funded directly from the state budget. Reports indicate the free service will be maintained on government funding through at least 2028.

Why now: the final piece of the sovereign AI drive

‘AI for Everyone’ did not come out of nowhere. It is closer to the last puzzle piece of the sovereign AI strategy the Korean government has been pushing for the past year.

  • The AI Basic Act (in force since January 2026) — the world’s second comprehensive AI law after the EU’s, framing AI as both a regulated technology and a national strategic asset.
  • The sovereign AI foundation-model project — a competitive program to raise “national champion” AI models. It started in August 2025 with five elite teams (SK Telecom, Naver Cloud, LG AI Research, Upstage, NC AI); in January’s first evaluation, LG AI Research (ranked first overall), SK Telecom, and Upstage advanced to stage two. Naver Cloud was cut for failing the weights-based independence criteria and NC AI on overall score — a demonstration of how strictly the government defines “domestic.”
  • A massive AI and semiconductor investment drive — in late June the government announced a landmark investment package spanning AI and chips, formalizing its “AI G3” (top-three AI power) ambition.

Seen in this sequence, the role of ‘AI for Everyone’ is obvious. Having built the law (the Basic Act) and the supply (sovereign models, GPUs, data centers), the government is now manufacturing demand — at the scale of the entire population.

Five things to watch

The announcement is dazzling; success will be decided by these five questions.

1. Can 512 B200s serve 52 million people? Bluntly: 512 GPUs is a very small number for an “unlimited” service open to an entire nation, especially compared with the tens or hundreds of thousands of GPUs global chatbot services burn. Realistically this means lightweight domestic models plus queues and rate limits when traffic spikes. The gap between the promise of “unlimited” and the actual user experience will define this service’s first impression.

2. Quality — the public already knows ChatGPT. The benchmark for this service isn’t “nothing”; it’s GPT-5.6, Claude, and Gemini. Korea’s top domestic models (LG’s Exaone among them) perform respectably on benchmarks, but a perceptible gap to frontier models remains. The moment this is perceived as “free but frustrating AI,” the project becomes a tax-money controversy. Conversely, if it can position itself as genuinely better than foreign models at Korean language, Korean bureaucracy, and Korean daily life, the story changes.

3. Sustainability — who pays after 2028? The free promise currently runs through 2028. Whether inference costs — plausibly in the tens of billions of won per year or more — keep being covered by taxpayers after that, or the service moves to partial paid tiers, is an open question. So is whether the structure can survive changes of government.

4. Data and privacy. The public-service agent understands your personal situation, proactively surfaces subsidies, and files applications for you. The flip side of that convenience: citizens’ everyday conversations and administrative data accumulate inside a government-funded service. Transparent rules on who holds the data, for how long, and for what uses are a precondition for trust.

5. Collision with the private market. When the government hands out free AI, the room for domestic paid AI services shrinks. Ironically, the companies joining this project may end up competing with their own paid offerings. On the other hand, participants gain something money can’t buy: nationwide traffic to learn and improve from. How each company weighs that dilemma will determine how hot the August tender gets.

The bottom line

‘AI for Everyone’ is a project with two faces. The front is a welfare service closing the “AI divide”; the back is industrial policy that hands domestic models scale demand in a home market dominated by foreign ones. Either way, a government distributing AI to its entire population as basic infrastructure has no precedent anywhere.

The late-September beta is the first test. What we see then may be a “Korean ChatGPT” — or a waiting queue balanced on 512 GPUs. Either way, the result of this experiment will be watched not just in Korea, but by every government quietly wondering whether to do the same.
